# The Only Son

## A Poem

In the house on the hillside where persimmons grow,  
one boy learned to carry what generations bestow—  
not just his name, but the names before,  
grandfather's hopes and the weight they bore.

His mother folded *hanbok* with careful hands,  
whispered dreams in a tongue that understands  
how a single son becomes a living bridge  
between the ancestors' graves and the mountain ridge.

*Adeul-ah,* they called—my son, my son—  
you are the continuation, the only one.  
At *doljanchi* they placed the thread, the book, the gold,  
choosing his future before stories could be told.

### Morning Lessons

While his cousins—daughters all—learned to yield,  
he sat at the table, his position sealed,  
first bowl of rice, the choicest piece of fish,  
uncomfortable crown in every dish.

His father's silence was a kind of speech,  
expectations floating just beyond his reach—  
Seoul National University, a respectable career,  
marriage, grandsons, keeping lineage clear.

### The Burden of Singular

He grew up knowing he could not be lost,  
could not rebel without unbearable cost.  
His sisters had each other's whispered jokes;  
he had the solitude that tradition evokes.

In *hagwon* rooms past midnight's chime,  
studying futures not entirely his to define,  
while his mother burned incense at the family shrine,  
praying he'd succeed on destiny's line.

### Between Two Worlds

At *Chuseok*, he bowed to ancestors' portraits lined,  
felt their eyes assess what they might find—  
would he honor what they'd built with calloused hands?  
Would he fulfill what duty demands?

But in secret moments, by the Han River's flow,  
he wondered who he'd be if he could simply grow  
into himself, unencumbered and free,  
not a branch, but a whole separate tree.

### The Modern Ache

His friends with siblings shared the load,  
split the inheritance, the ancestral road.  
But he alone would tend his parents' aging years,  
manage their hopes, their health, their fears.

Korea changing faster than tradition bends—  
skyscrapers rising where rice paddies end,  
yet in his chest, the old and new collide:  
*filial duty* and the urge to decide.

### The Question He Carries

What if he disappoints? What if he fails?  
The only son—where going off-trail  
means not just his falling, but a lineage undone,  
centuries of continuity in a single son.

And yet—

### And Yet

There is honor in the carrying, too,  
in being the one they turn to,  
in grandmother's hand upon his head,  
in the unspoken love in what's left unsaid.

The only son is not only burden's child,  
but also the beloved, the precious, the reconciled.  
His existence itself a kind of poem,  
written in the space between duty and home.

## Epilogue: The Man He Becomes

Perhaps he'll find a way to be  
both keeper of tradition and utterly free,  
to honor where he's from while choosing where he'll go—  
the only son, in Seoul's neon glow,

bowing to ancestors on holy days,  
but walking forward in his own ways,  
carrying the names, yes, but also his own story,  
a singular life, complex and contradictory.

In the house on the hillside where persimmons grow,  
he learns what only sons eventually know:  
You can be the bridge and still be whole,  
tradition's vessel with an individual soul.

---

*For every only son who has learned to balance*  
*the weight of lineage with the lightness of becoming*
